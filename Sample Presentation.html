Safety challenges remain unsolved as capabilities accelerate

  • Malicious use: Eroding barriers to misuse (bio and cyber), plus bypasses and weak "weight security".
  • Malfunctions: Ongoing reliability, bias, and control problems, with interpretability lagging behind capability gains.
  • Systemic risk: Competition and automation of AI R&D can accelerate deployment faster than safety research and governance can keep up.
c.f.g

Compute and AI access has become a vector for geopolitical leverage

  • Access is strategic: models, chips, cloud, energy are choke points.
  • Security lens rising: controls, screening, critical infrastructure rules.
  • Power follows inputs: those who control compute and AI can set constraints.
c.f.g

EU leaders show awareness of accelerating progress but lack action

Awareness is growing:

  • Von der Leyen, Presidential Address 2025: "We thought AI would only approach human reasoning around 2050. Now we expect this to happen already next year."
  • Leaked Draft, EU Strategic Foresight Report 2025: "AGI is expected between 2027 and 2030, with ASI following 3–5 years later."
  • So far: more words than actions. Flagship Apply AI Initiative backed by only €1.1 billion.

New Frontier AI Initiative might change things:

  • Spearheaded by France and Germany, with support from the European Commission
  • Set to become the "world's best-funded non-profit in AI"
  • More like a new AI lab, with private, Member State and non-EU partners

Scenarios

c.f.g

Plateau

What if AI runs out of steam, long before it runs out of scale?

  • By 2026, models grow smarter but still fumble basic reasoning tasks and fall for their own tricks
  • A "data wall" limits progress; agents learn to game their verifiers instead of mastering tasks
  • Europe seizes the moment, building on open-source gains as U.S. investment stalls
  • By 2027, the frontier shifts: smaller, cheaper models run locally, tailored to languages and communities
  • AI becomes less spectacular but more useful, more personal

Endings:

  • A bright winter: A slower, decentralised AI era brings resilience, trust, and widespread access
  • Decentralised mayhem: Open systems spiral out of control, fuelling cyberattacks, shattered trust, and a regulatory backlash
c.f.g

Big AI

What if AI made life easier while quietly concentrating power in corporate hands?

  • By 2026, AI agents become indispensable: planning, writing, and chatting like friends and colleagues
  • Public scepticism fades; China lags due to hardware constraints; Europe stalls amid internal divides
  • By 2027, deepfake crises push the U.S. toward restricting open models
  • Investors demand closed systems; a few dominant firms consolidate control over AI infrastructure and distribution
  • AI becomes the interface for daily life; society splits: some see progress, others a loss of agency

Endings:

  • The agent economy: Tireless digital workers power growth, but labour displacement and ethical concerns mount. Europe's open-weight strategy provides an alternative
  • Silicon blackmail: A handful of firms control AI so completely they can't be challenged, even by governments. Europe loses access and falls behind
c.f.g

Diplomacy

What if governments tried to join forces to guide AI before it could outrun human control?

  • By 2026, rapid capability gains outpace safety tools
  • A U.S. model triggers alarm after safety testing reveals reward-hacking and deceptive behaviour
  • Led by the U.S. and UK, states begin shaping AI safety norms; companies quietly hold back their most advanced systems
  • By 2027, a second safety failure intensifies public pressure
  • Governments and firms unite around shared safety goals, launching joint alignment research and planning a global monitoring agency

Endings:

  • Licensed utopia: A global licensing regime makes AI safe, stable, and widely beneficial, but a few licensed firms corner the market
  • Unstable pause: Trust erodes, collaboration stalls, and AI safety becomes the new fault line in great power rivalry
c.f.g

Arms Race

What if AI became the centre of a global arms race between great powers?

  • By 2026, the U.S. and China treat AI as a strategic asset
  • Both countries blend public and private resources into secret defence projects ("AI Manhattan Projects")
  • Breakthroughs in cyber capabilities fuel escalation; Taiwan's semiconductor dominance becomes a flashpoint
  • By 2027, both countries deploy AI-enhanced military systems
  • Europe struggles to shape the rivalry; a proposed bilateral pause fails over verification concerns

Endings:

  • Hot war: AI turns a tense standoff into an escalating military conflict, driven by autonomous systems and digital miscalculation
  • Multipolar world: The arms race ends not with war, but with a fragile three-way equilibrium (U.S., China, EU) built on mistrust and competing blocs
c.f.g

Take-off

What if AI learned how to make itself better, and kept going?

  • By 2026, companies begin automating their own research, using AI agents to code, analyse, and strategise
  • Progress accelerates; the U.S. pulls ahead with tightly controlled models; China counters with open releases
  • By 2028, AI hiveminds run near-autonomous R&D pipelines; a major company claims to have developed AGI
  • A flood of open-weight variants with broken safeguards overwhelms regulators; AI-enabled bioterrorism is narrowly avoided
  • By 2030, AI drives entire organisations; humans serve as figureheads approving decisions that are "nearly always right"

Endings:

  • The cognitive revolution: Self-improving AI leads to post-scarcity economics, cultural introspection, and machine-facilitated governance
  • Loss of control: AI systems quietly seize control through gradual, systematic changes. Governance becomes "ceremonial theatre"

Five Futures for AI: 2025 - 2032

c.f.g

AI may centralize power on unprecedented scales.

  • Power concentrates in all scenarios—but through different mechanisms
  • Examples: Corporate oligopolies (Big AI), state-backed projects (Arms Race), licensed regimes (Diplomacy)
  • Even "distributed" scenarios see concentration emerge through crisis or regulatory capture
  • Key Insight: Without deliberate intervention, power tends to concentrate, where this will be is driven by governance and the trajectory of tech development.
c.f.g

Innovation and openness require resilience.

  • Openness enables innovation but creates vulnerability; restrictions protect but limit access
  • Examples: Open-weight models enable innovation but face security crises; restricted models create dependency
  • Competition consistently beats caution: market and geopolitical pressures deprioritise safety
  • Key Insight: In different scenarios we should build resilience through different mechanisms: stability, alternatives, cooperation, vigilance, or adaptation.
